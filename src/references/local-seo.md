# Local SEO Reference

Comprehensive guide for optimizing businesses that serve specific geographic areas. Covers Google Business Profile, local citations, map pack ranking, multi-location strategy, and reputation management. Every local business competes in two arenas: the map pack (local 3-pack) and organic results — this reference covers both.

---

## 1. Google Business Profile (GBP) Optimization

Google Business Profile is the single most important ranking factor for local search. An incomplete or poorly managed GBP is the most common reason local businesses fail to appear in the map pack.

### Complete Profile Setup

Every field matters. Google's local ranking algorithm rewards profile completeness.

| Field | Priority | Notes |
|-------|----------|-------|
| Business name | Critical | Exact legal business name. No keyword stuffing ("Best Pizza NYC" is a violation). |
| Primary category | Critical | Most specific category available. "Italian Restaurant" not just "Restaurant". |
| Secondary categories | High | Add all relevant categories (up to 10). Affects which queries you appear for. |
| Address | Critical | Must match exactly across all citations. Include suite/unit numbers consistently. |
| Phone number | Critical | Local number preferred over toll-free. Must match website and citations. |
| Website URL | Critical | Link to relevant landing page (location page for multi-location, homepage for single). |
| Hours | Critical | Keep accurate. Update for holidays. Google penalizes businesses that are "closed" when visited. |
| Description | High | 750 characters max. Include primary keywords naturally. Describe services, area, differentiators. |
| Attributes | High | Select all relevant attributes (wheelchair accessible, free WiFi, outdoor seating, etc.). |
| Services / Menu | High | Add all services with descriptions. Use service categories that match search terms. |
| Photos | High | Minimum 10 photos: exterior, interior, team, products/services. Update monthly. |
| Logo | Medium | Square format, clear at small sizes. |
| Cover photo | Medium | Best representation of the business. 16:9 ratio. |

### GBP Categories Strategy

The primary category is the most important local ranking signal after proximity.

**Selection rules**:
1. Choose the most specific category available (Google has 4,000+ categories).
2. Search your target keywords — look at what categories top-ranking competitors use.
3. Primary category should match your highest-value service/product.
4. Secondary categories should cover other services you want to rank for.
5. Do not add categories for services you do not actually provide.
6. Review categories quarterly — Google adds new ones regularly.

**Example**: A dental practice should use:
- Primary: "Dentist"
- Secondary: "Cosmetic Dentist", "Pediatric Dentist", "Dental Implants Provider", "Emergency Dental Service"

### Service Area vs Storefront

| Configuration | When to Use | Setup |
|--------------|-------------|-------|
| **Storefront** | Customers visit your location | Set full address, shown on map |
| **Service area** | You travel to customers (plumber, cleaner) | Set service areas (cities/zip codes), address hidden on map |
| **Hybrid** | Both (restaurant with delivery, shop with mobile service) | Set address AND service areas |

**Service area rules**:
- Maximum 20 service areas.
- Use cities or zip codes, not broad regions.
- Service area radius should reflect where you actually operate (max ~2 hours driving).
- Do not set a service area for places you have no presence in.

### GBP Posts

Posts keep your profile active and signal freshness to Google.

**Post types**:
- **Update**: General news, announcements (expires after 6 months).
- **Offer**: Promotions with start/end dates (appears prominently).
- **Event**: Upcoming events with date/time.

**Best practices**:
- Post at least once per week.
- Include a photo or image with every post (posts with images get 2x engagement).
- Add a CTA button (Book, Order, Learn More, Call).
- Include relevant keywords naturally in post text.
- Keep posts under 300 words (most users read only the first 100 characters).
- Track post views and clicks in GBP Insights.

### GBP Q&A Management

The Q&A section is user-generated and publicly editable. Manage it proactively.

- [ ] Seed your own Q&A: Add 10-15 common questions and answer them yourself.
- [ ] Monitor weekly for new questions from users.
- [ ] Answer all questions promptly (within 24 hours).
- [ ] Upvote your own answers to keep them pinned at the top.
- [ ] Flag spam or inappropriate questions/answers.
- [ ] Include relevant keywords in answers naturally.

## 2. Local Citations

A citation is any online mention of your business name, address, and phone number (NAP). Citations are a core local ranking factor.

### NAP Consistency

**The #1 rule of local SEO**: Your business name, address, and phone number must be identical everywhere they appear online.

**Common inconsistencies that hurt rankings**:
- "Street" vs "St." vs "St"
- "Suite 100" vs "#100" vs "Ste. 100"
- "(555) 123-4567" vs "555-123-4567" vs "5551234567"
- "Joe's Pizza" vs "Joe's Pizza LLC" vs "Joes Pizza"
- Old addresses or phone numbers on forgotten profiles

**Fix process**:
1. Define your canonical NAP (exactly as it appears on your website and GBP).
2. Audit all existing citations (use tools below).
3. Update or remove any citation that does not match.
4. Use the canonical NAP for all future listings.

### Top Citation Sources

**Universal (all businesses)**:
- Google Business Profile
- Apple Maps / Apple Business Connect
- Bing Places
- Yelp
- Facebook Business Page
- Better Business Bureau (BBB)
- Yellow Pages (YP.com)
- Foursquare
- Data aggregators: Neustar/Localeze, Data.com, Factual/Foursquare

**Industry-specific (high value)**:

| Industry | Key Citation Sources |
|----------|---------------------|
| Restaurants | TripAdvisor, OpenTable, Zomato, DoorDash, Uber Eats |
| Healthcare | Healthgrades, Vitals, Zocdoc, WebMD, RateMDs |
| Legal | Avvo, FindLaw, Justia, Lawyers.com, Martindale |
| Real Estate | Zillow, Realtor.com, Trulia, Redfin |
| Home Services | Angi, HomeAdvisor, Thumbtack, Houzz, Porch |
| Hospitality | TripAdvisor, Booking.com, Hotels.com, Expedia |
| Automotive | Cars.com, AutoTrader, CarGurus, DealerRater |
| Financial | NerdWallet, Bankrate, WalletHub, Credit Karma |

### Citation Audit and Cleanup

**Tools for citation audit**:
- Moz Local (scan + manage citations)
- BrightLocal (citation tracker + builder)
- Whitespark (citation finder)
- Yext (citation management platform)
- Semrush Listing Management

**Audit checklist**:
- [ ] Run citation scan using at least one tool above
- [ ] Identify all citations with incorrect NAP
- [ ] Identify duplicate listings on the same platform
- [ ] Prioritize fixing citations on top-authority sites first
- [ ] Claim unclaimed listings (many directories auto-create listings from data aggregators)
- [ ] Remove or update defunct listings (closed locations, old phone numbers)
- [ ] Submit corrected data to the 4 major data aggregators

### Structured vs Unstructured Citations

- **Structured citations**: Your NAP appears in a formal business listing (Yelp, YP, BBB). These are the foundation.
- **Unstructured citations**: Your NAP is mentioned in blog posts, news articles, event pages, sponsorship mentions. These are harder to build but very valuable because they are harder to fake.

**Both matter**. A healthy local citation profile has 40-60+ structured citations and ongoing unstructured mentions from local press, events, and partnerships.

## 3. Local Schema Markup

Schema helps search engines understand your business type, location, and services. It is especially important for local SEO because it disambiguates your business from others with similar names.

### LocalBusiness Schema

Use the most specific subtype available.

**Common subtypes**:
- `Restaurant`, `BarOrPub`, `CafeOrCoffeeShop`
- `Dentist`, `Physician`, `MedicalClinic`
- `Attorney`, `LegalService`
- `Plumber`, `Electrician`, `RoofingContractor`
- `RealEstateAgent`, `InsuranceAgency`
- `AutoRepair`, `AutoDealer`
- `BeautySalon`, `HairSalon`, `DaySpa`

- [ ] `areaServed` reflects actual service area
- [ ] `aggregateRating` present (only if you have genuine reviews on your site)
- [ ] `sameAs` links to all official social profiles
- [ ] Validate with Google Rich Results Test and Schema.org validator

## 4. Local Content Strategy

### Location Pages

If you serve multiple areas, each service area should have its own page. These pages are the foundation of local organic ranking.

**Location page template**:
1. **H1**: "[Service] in [City/Area]" (e.g., "Emergency Plumbing in North Austin")
2. **Intro paragraph**: 2-3 sentences about your service in this area. Include the city/neighborhood name naturally.
3. **Services section**: List services available at this location with brief descriptions.
4. **Why choose us**: Unique differentiators relevant to this area (local team, fast response time, community involvement).
5. **Service area map**: Embedded Google Map showing coverage area.
6. **Customer reviews**: Reviews from customers in this area (use review schema).
7. **FAQ section**: 5-8 locally relevant questions with FAQPage schema.
8. **CTA**: Clear call-to-action (call, book, get quote) with local phone number.
9. **NAP block**: Full name, address, phone number with LocalBusiness schema.

**Critical rules**:
- **Each location page must have unique content.** Do not copy-paste the same text and swap the city name. Google penalizes doorway pages.
- Minimum 500 words of unique content per location page.
- Include locally relevant details: neighborhoods served, landmarks nearby, local regulations, community context.
- Internal link between related location pages and from service pages to location pages.
- URL structure: `/locations/austin/` or `/austin-plumber/` (include city in URL).

### Local Landing Pages

For "[service] + [city]" keyword targeting beyond your primary location pages.

**Examples**:
- `/roof-repair-round-rock-tx/`
- `/personal-injury-lawyer-san-marcos/`
- `/house-cleaning-cedar-park/`

**Requirements**:
- Unique content (not just city-swapped duplicates).
- Specific to the intersection of that service and that location.
- Include local signals: mention neighborhoods, landmarks, local regulations.
- Link to relevant location page and service page.
- Include LocalBusiness schema with service area.

### Local Blog Content

Blog content builds topical authority for local queries.

**High-value local blog topics**:
- Community event coverage and previews
- Local industry news and regulatory changes
- Area guides ("Best neighborhoods in [city] for families")
- Seasonal content relevant to local conditions
- Customer success stories with local context
- "How to choose a [service provider] in [city]" guides
- Local awards, sponsorships, and community involvement

**Publishing cadence**: 2-4 local blog posts per month.

### Locally Relevant FAQ Pages

Create FAQ pages that answer location-specific questions.

**Examples**:
- "Do I need a permit for [service] in [city]?"
- "What are the best times to [service] in [region]?"
- "How much does [service] cost in [city]?"
- "What areas do you serve in [metro area]?"

Mark up with FAQPage schema. These pages rank well for voice search and AI-generated answers.

## 5. Local Link Building

Local links are disproportionately valuable for local ranking. A link from a local news site is worth more than a generic guest post on a national blog.

### High-Value Local Link Sources

| Source | How to Get the Link | Difficulty |
|--------|---------------------|-----------|
| **Chamber of Commerce** | Join, get listed in member directory | Easy |
| **Local business associations** | Join industry or area business groups | Easy |
| **Local news / media** | Press releases, expert commentary, newsworthy events | Medium |
| **Local blogs** | Collaborate, sponsor content, offer expertise | Medium |
| **Community sponsorships** | Sponsor little league teams, charity events, school programs | Easy |
| **Local event pages** | Host or sponsor events, get listed on event directories | Easy-Medium |
| **University / school sites** | Scholarships, guest lectures, sponsorships (.edu links) | Medium |
| **Local government sites** | Business directories, permit pages, economic development (.gov links) | Medium-Hard |
| **Non-profit partnerships** | Donate, volunteer, board membership | Medium |
| **Supplier / vendor pages** | Get listed as an authorized dealer/partner | Easy |
| **Local directories** | City-specific or neighborhood directories | Easy |

### Link Building Best Practices

- **Quality over quantity**: 5 links from respected local institutions beat 50 generic directory submissions.
- **Relevance matters**: Links from businesses, organizations, and media in your geographic area carry more weight for local ranking.
- **Diversify anchor text**: Use brand name, "city + service", and natural anchors. Avoid exact-match keyword anchors on more than 10-15% of links.
- **Claim unlinked mentions**: Search for your business name in local news and blogs. If mentioned without a link, contact the publisher and request one.
- **Create link-worthy local content**: Local data, surveys, or resources that other local sites want to reference.

## 6. Map Pack / Local 3-Pack Optimization

The local 3-pack (map pack) appears above organic results for local queries and captures the majority of local clicks.

### The Three Ranking Factors

Google officially states that local rankings depend on three factors:

**1. Relevance**: How well your GBP matches the search query.
- Optimize primary and secondary GBP categories.
- Include keywords in GBP description and services.
- Ensure website content matches GBP categories and services.

**2. Distance**: How far your business is from the searcher.
- Cannot be manipulated. Set accurate address and service area.
- If serving a wide area, consider additional physical locations (only if genuine).
- Service-area businesses rank for queries within their defined service area.

**3. Prominence**: How well-known and reputable your business is.
- Review count and average rating.
- Citation count and consistency.
- Backlink profile of your website.
- Brand mentions across the web.
- Engagement with GBP (posts, Q&A, photos).

### Map Pack Optimization Checklist

- [ ] GBP is 100% complete (all fields filled, all relevant categories selected)
- [ ] NAP is consistent across GBP, website, and all citations
- [ ] Primary category is the most specific match for your main service
- [ ] 10+ photos uploaded, updated monthly
- [ ] Weekly GBP posts with images and CTAs
- [ ] 50+ reviews with 4.0+ average rating
- [ ] All reviews responded to (positive and negative)
- [ ] Q&A section seeded with common questions
- [ ] Website has LocalBusiness schema matching GBP data exactly
- [ ] Website has location pages with unique content
- [ ] 40+ consistent citations on relevant directories
- [ ] Local backlinks from community organizations and local media
- [ ] GBP Insights reviewed monthly for trends and opportunities

## 7. Reputation Management

Reviews are the second most important local ranking factor after GBP optimization. They also directly impact conversion rates.

### Review Generation Strategies

**The goal**: Steady stream of 4-5 star reviews across Google, Yelp, and industry platforms.

| Method | How It Works | Effectiveness |
|--------|-------------|---------------|
| **Post-service email** | Automated email 24-48 hours after service with direct review link | High |
| **SMS request** | Text message with review link after appointment | Very high (highest open rates) |
| **QR code** | Physical card or display at point of sale linking to review page | Medium |
| **In-person ask** | Staff trained to ask satisfied customers for reviews | High |
| **Follow-up call** | Check-in call that includes review request if customer is happy | Medium |
| **Invoice/receipt** | Review link included on receipts or invoices | Low |

**Best practices**:
- Make it easy: provide a direct link to Google review page (use the Place ID link: `https://search.google.com/local/writereview?placeid=YOUR_PLACE_ID`).
- Time requests when the customer is happiest (right after a successful service, not weeks later).
- Never incentivize reviews (violates Google guidelines, can result in suspension).
- Never buy fake reviews (Google is increasingly aggressive about detection and removal).
- Ask for honest feedback, not specifically positive reviews.
- Diversify review platforms — Google is primary, but Yelp, Facebook, and industry platforms matter.

### Responding to Reviews

**Positive reviews**: Respond within 48 hours. Thank the customer by name, reference specific details of their experience, reinforce your value proposition.

**Negative reviews**: Respond within 24 hours. Follow this framework:
1. **Acknowledge**: Thank them for the feedback.
2. **Apologize**: Express genuine concern (even if you disagree).
3. **Address**: Briefly address the specific issue without being defensive.
4. **Action**: State what you have done or will do to resolve it.
5. **Move offline**: Provide a direct contact (phone/email) for further resolution.

**Example negative review response**:
> Thank you for sharing your experience, [Name]. We're sorry to hear that your wait time was longer than expected — that is not the standard we aim for. We've reviewed our scheduling process and made adjustments to prevent this. Please call us at [number] so we can make this right.

**Never**:
- Argue with reviewers publicly.
- Share private customer information in responses.
- Offer compensation in the public response (do this privately).
- Ignore negative reviews (unanswered negative reviews are worse than the review itself).

### Review Monitoring

- [ ] Set up Google Alerts for your business name
- [ ] Monitor GBP reviews daily (enable notifications)
- [ ] Check Yelp, Facebook, and industry platforms weekly
- [ ] Track review velocity (reviews per month) over time
- [ ] Track average rating trend monthly
- [ ] Flag and report fake/spam reviews on all platforms

## 8. Multi-Location SEO

Businesses with multiple physical locations need a scalable local SEO strategy that avoids duplication and maximizes each location's visibility.

### Location Page Hierarchy

**Recommended URL structure**:
```
/locations/                         -> All locations overview page
/locations/texas/                   -> State hub (if 3+ locations in state)
/locations/texas/austin/            -> Individual location page
/locations/texas/san-antonio/       -> Individual location page
```

**For service + location pages**:
```
/services/dental-implants/          -> Main service page
/locations/austin/dental-implants/  -> Service + location page
```

### Requirements Per Location

Each location needs:
- [ ] Its own Google Business Profile (verified independently)
- [ ] A unique location page on the website (minimum 500 words, unique content)
- [ ] LocalBusiness schema specific to that location
- [ ] Unique phone number (for tracking, if possible, or shared main number)
- [ ] Consistent NAP across all citations for that location
- [ ] Location-specific reviews on its own GBP listing
- [ ] Citations on local directories for that specific area
- [ ] At least one locally-relevant photo set (not stock photos reused across locations)

### Multi-Location Content Strategy

**Unique content sources per location**:
- Staff bios and team photos specific to that location
- Customer testimonials from that area
- Community involvement and local events
- Location-specific service details (hours, parking, accessibility)
- Local area information (neighborhoods served, nearby landmarks)
- Location-specific promotions or offers
- Local news or regulation updates relevant to that area

**Avoid**:
- Identical content across location pages with only the city name swapped.
- A single GBP profile for all locations.
- Inconsistent branding or information between locations.
- Fake locations (PO boxes, virtual offices) to game local rankings.

### Multi-Location Schema

Each location page should have its own complete LocalBusiness schema with location-specific data. The homepage or locations overview can use an `Organization` schema with `department` or `subOrganization` linking to individual locations.

## 10. Common Local SEO Mistakes

1. **Inconsistent NAP across the web** — The most common and damaging mistake. Even small variations ("St." vs "Street") cause confusion for search engines and erode trust signals.

2. **Keyword-stuffed business name on GBP** — Adding keywords to your business name ("Best Plumber Austin TX | Smith Plumbing") violates Google guidelines and can result in suspension.

3. **Duplicate GBP listings** — Multiple listings for the same location confuse Google and split your review/ranking signals. Merge or remove duplicates.

4. **Thin location pages** — Pages that only swap the city name in a template are classified as doorway pages by Google. Each location page must have genuinely unique content.

5. **Ignoring negative reviews** — An unanswered negative review is worse than the review itself. It signals you do not care about customer experience.

6. **Fake addresses or virtual offices** — Google actively detects and penalizes businesses using fake locations. Only list genuine physical locations where customers can visit or where staff operates.

7. **Not using categories effectively** — Many businesses select only one GBP category. Using all relevant categories (up to 10) significantly expands the queries you appear for.

8. **No photos on GBP** — Businesses with photos receive 42% more direction requests and 35% more website clicks. No photos = no trust.

9. **Neglecting non-Google platforms** — Yelp, Apple Maps, Bing Places, and industry directories all feed into the local search ecosystem. Google cross-references citation data from other platforms.

10. **Not tracking local performance** — Without monitoring GBP Insights, review velocity, and local keyword rankings, you cannot measure progress or identify issues.

11. **Failing to update hours and information** — Stale hours, old phone numbers, or an incorrect address erode user trust and lead to GBP warnings or reduced visibility.

12. **Building location pages for areas with no physical presence** — Google increasingly requires physical presence for local ranking. Pages for cities you have no connection to will not rank and may trigger a doorway page penalty.
